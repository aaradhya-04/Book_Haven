Let's find your favourite book!

  1. After Lives

    2. “Afterlives” by Abdulrazak Gurnah is a novel that delves into the lives of two African men serving in the German colonial forces during World War I. The book explores how our paths are shaped by chance encounters and the forces around us. It’s a story of survival, kindness, and the indelible marks left by history. If you are interested in narratives that blend beauty and tragedy, this one might resonate with you.


  2. An Immense World

    3. This book is filled with strange creatures and strange experiments; Yong is interested in what animals perceive, what they might communicate to us if they could. Humans see the world one way. Other species see it through very different eyes, and many don’t see it at all. Attempting to exchange one world view—or, to use the term Yong favors, Umwelt—for another may be frustrating, but, he argues, that’s what makes the effort worthwhile. It reminds us that, “for all our vaunted intelligence,” our Umwelt is just one among millions. Some species of scallop, for instance, have dozens of eyes; others have hundreds. We can learn a lot from the methods that animals use to sense their surroundings. And doing so can be, for us, mind-expanding.


  3. Bad Mexicans

    4. The nickname malos Mexicanos, translated in the title of this captivating history, is what the Mexican dictator Porfirio Díaz called the followers of the radical Ricardo Flores Magón, who, in 1911, helped depose him. The author, a U.C.L.A. historian and a MacArthur Fellow, writes that Magón and his band of magonistas “changed the course of history both north and south of the border.” She shows how their revolution fundamentally transformed the United States, as more than a million Mexicans migrated north. Although few Americans know about the event or the people behind it, Lytle Hernández argues powerfully that “you cannot understand U.S. history without Mexico and Mexicans.


  4. Ikigai

    5. Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life* by Hector Garcia and Francesc Miralles explores the Japanese concept of "ikigai," meaning "a reason for being." The book delves into how finding one's ikigai can lead to a fulfilling and meaningful life. Drawing on interviews with Japanese centenarians and insights from the fields of psychology and sociology, it offers practical advice on living with purpose and joy. With its blend of cultural wisdom and practical tips, Ikigai is an inspiring guide to achieving long-lasting happiness and fulfillment.


  5. Sherlock Holmes

    6. The Sherlock Holmes series by Sir Arthur Conan Doyle is a collection of detective stories that follow the brilliant and eccentric detective Sherlock Holmes and his loyal friend Dr. John Watson. Set in Victorian London, the series is renowned for its intricate plots, keen observations, and Holmes's extraordinary deductive abilities. Each story showcases Holmes solving complex mysteries with his sharp intellect and keen eye for detail. The timeless appeal of these stories lies in their masterful storytelling, memorable characters, and the enduring fascination with the art of deduction.


  6. Checkout 19

    7. A coming-of-age story in which no one comes of age, this novel is deliberate in its construction and yet aggressively resistant to definition. Dissected, it yields a conventional enough story: that of an intelligent working-class girl, in southwest England, who is encouraged to write by one of her teachers. For good and for ill, she both clings to and disowns her life. But Bennett is interested not in the shape of a life but in its substance: the prized darkness at the center of the human mind, the place where whatever is really real about us resides.


  7. Rich Dad Poor Dad

    8. Rich Dad Poor Dad by Robert Kiyosaki is a bestselling personal finance book that contrasts the financial philosophies of the author's two "dads"—his real father (Poor Dad) and the father of his best friend (Rich Dad). Through their differing approaches to money, Kiyosaki illustrates key principles of financial literacy, including the importance of investing, understanding assets and liabilities, and the value of financial education. The book's accessible style and practical advice have made it a popular guide for those seeking to improve their financial acumen and achieve financial independence.


  8. The Time Traveller

    9. The Time Traveller by J. Kreisch is a captivating science fiction novel that follows the journey of a protagonist who discovers the ability to travel through time. As they navigate different eras, the story explores the complexities and consequences of altering historical events. With its intriguing plot and thought-provoking themes, The Time Traveller delves into the ethical dilemmas and paradoxes associated with time travel. Kreisch's engaging writing and imaginative storytelling make this book a compelling read for fans of the genre.


  9. Getting Lost

    10. Ernaux’s 1991 best-seller “Simple Passion,” a short narrative of her affair with A., as she calls her lover, conveyed the force with which desire can render the rest of life—the rest of the self—instantly void. A decade later, Ernaux did something surprising and published excerpts of the diary she had kept during the affair, which was released in the U.S. in September. Here is the sex and the torture of the waiting, unspooled in all its real-time wretchedness. Now called by his true initial, S., her lover turns out to be a thirty-five-year-old Soviet apparatchik whom the forty-eight-year-old Ernaux met on a writer’s junket to the U.S.S.R. The diary depicts the friction between her finely developed mind and the tyrannical demands of her body, and the gulf between her devotion to her lover and her awareness of his obvious mediocrity.


  10. Deathnote

    11. DeathNote by Tsugumi Ohba and Takeshi Obata is a gripping manga series about a high school student, Light Yagami, who discovers a mysterious notebook that allows him to kill anyone whose name he writes in it. As Light takes on the persona of "Kira" to rid the world of criminals, he faces off against the brilliant detective "L" in a high-stakes battle of wits. With its dark themes, complex moral dilemmas, and suspenseful plot twists, Death Note is a compelling and thought-provoking read that explores the nature of justice and power.


  11. Think and Grow Rich

    12. Think and Grow Rich by Napoleon Hill is a classic self-help book focused on achieving personal and financial success. Based on Hill's study of successful individuals, the book outlines key principles for wealth creation, including the power of desire, belief, and persistence. Through practical advice and inspirational stories, Hill encourages readers to harness their thoughts and ambitions to achieve their goals. With its enduring wisdom and motivational message, *Think and Grow Rich* remains a cornerstone in the personal development genre.


  12. Magnificent Rebels

    13. A buoyant work of intellectual history, Wulf’s chronicle of the early Romantics—which includes figures like the Schlegel brothers (August Wilhelm and Friedrich), and their partners, Caroline and Dorothea—is written as what was once termed the “higher gossip.” It concerns a period, in the late seventeen-nineties and early eighteen hundreds, when Jena, a small university town in Saxony, became home to a literary cenacle of men and women who “placed the self at the centre stage of their thinking.” They believed in free love and self-expression—although the women’s contributions were typically credited to the men in their lives. A steady and ominous undertone to all the cogitation and copulation is the rise of Napoleon from the ashes of the 1789 revolution in France to a conclusive military blow against the Germans that was also centered in the town: the Battle of Jena.
